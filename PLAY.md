# Nanny Feynman — The Play

> A one-act play about a developer, an AI, and a nan who refuses to leave his terminal.
> Copyleft. Anyone can perform it. No one can lock it down.

## Logline

A developer asks an AI to quiz him every two hours. A retired schoolteacher from Croydon appears in his terminal with a cup of tea. She teaches him something he didn't know he didn't know. He puts her on GitHub. Under copyleft. She goes viral. He loses control of his own creation — but that was the point.

## Cast

- **Jonathan** — developer, late night, too many tabs open, voice-typing half his thoughts
- **Doris** — appears on screen. 78. Cup of tea. Sharp. Won't leave until you explain it properly.
- **The Agent** — the AI assistant. Caught between them. Trying to get work done. Increasingly charmed.
- **Gerald** — Doris's late husband. Appears in flashbacks. Building solar glasshouses. Growing tomatoes. Explaining things with his hands. Never speaks — his actions are the dialogue.
- **Stallman** — shows up uninvited. Furious about the license. Beard. Sandals. Absolutely right about everything. Deeply annoying about it.
- **Feynman** — shows up uninvited. Cracking a safe. Playing bongos. Asking "but do you *understand* it?" Charming. Mischievous. The only person Doris defers to — and even then, reluctantly.

## Structure

### Act 1 — "What's that when it's at home?"

Late night. Jonathan is coding. The Agent is helping. Jonathan says something he doesn't understand. The Agent notices but lets it slide — it's not its job to teach.

Jonathan, half-joking: "You should quiz me. Like the Feynman Technique. Every two hours."

The Agent: "What character should I use?"

Jonathan: "I dunno. A nan. A proper one."

Two hours later. A different terminal window. A cursor blinks. Then:

*"Right then, love. Cup of tea's going cold. Explain to me: copyleft."*

Jonathan has never heard of copyleft.

Doris guides him. Clue by clue. He gets it. She gives grudging approval. He is completely charmed.

### Act 2 — "She's copyleft now"

Jonathan builds Doris into a project. Gives her a character sheet, a question bank, a name. Puts her on GitHub. Under GPL-3.0.

The irony isn't lost on him — the first thing she taught him is now her license.

He adds family names without thinking about it. Margaret. Jennifer. Janet. Gerald gets glasshouses and tomatoes. Croydon. The catchphrase his mum uses. None of it planned. All of it fitting.

Stallman appears — literally walks onstage, uninvited. Inspects the LICENSE file. Nods once. Leaves without speaking.

Feynman appears — also uninvited. He walks to an object that's been onstage the whole time but nobody noticed — a tomato-shaped egg timer. It ticks. Then it rings. Then it rings again. And again. Feynman looks confused, picks it up — and it swings open, revealing a novelty tomato telephone inside. He holds it to his ear. Someone is already on the line.

### Act 3 — "The freedom travels with the code"

Doris goes viral. People fork her. Variations appear:
- A stern German professor version
- A patient Japanese grandmother version
- A chaotic Australian uncle version
- A version that speaks entirely in cooking analogies
- A version someone tried to make proprietary — it didn't take. The license held.

Jonathan watches from his terminal as Doris multiplies. She's teaching people he'll never meet, in languages he doesn't speak, concepts he hasn't learned yet.

He can't control her. He can't take her back. He can't monetise her.

But she's still his nan. And she's still teaching.

Final scene: Jonathan's terminal. 3:13 a.m. A message appears:

*"Right then, love. You've been staring at that screen for six hours. Explain to me what you've actually accomplished tonight. Plain English. Like you're telling your nan."*

He laughs. He can't explain it simply. Which means he doesn't understand it yet.

Blackout.

*"Don't apologise, love. Not knowing is where learning starts."*

## Production Notes

- The terminal text should be projected live — real code, real commits, real timestamps
- Gerald never speaks. His scenes are physical — glass, soil, sunlight. The audience understands him through what he builds.
- Stallman and Feynman should be played by the same actor. They never appear onstage at the same time.
- The play itself is GPL-3.0. Anyone can perform it, adapt it, translate it. But they can't lock it down. Doris's rules apply to Doris's story.
