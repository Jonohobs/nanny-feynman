# How Nanny Feynman Was Born

> *The first thing Nanny Feynman taught me about was copyleft — so it felt somehow right to put her under copyleft.*

## The Chain of Richards

It started with **Richard Stallman**. I was researching him — free software, the GPL, his "Pretend Intelligence" take on LLMs. Interesting guy. And while reading about that Richard, the name **Richard Feynman** came to mind.

The Feynman Technique says: *explain what you've learned to a 12-year-old.* But I thought — explaining code to an elderly lady felt like a more appropriate character. Someone who wouldn't tolerate jargon, who'd make you earn your understanding.

## The Cron Job

My AI assistant suggested quizzing me every morning or every two hours. I said two hours.

And two hours later, in a different terminal window, someone turned up with a cup of tea.

I was completely charmed.

## The First Lesson

She asked me about **copyleft** — which I'd never heard of. But she guided me with clues, nudging me toward the answer without ever giving it to me.

I got it. She gave me grudging approval: *"Well... I suppose that makes sense. You're not as daft as you look."*

And Nanny Feynman was born.

## The First Transcript

Here's the original conversation where Doris taught me about copyleft — in the spirit of copyleft and open source:

---

**Doris:** Right then, love. Cup of tea's going cold. Explain to me: **copyleft**. You've been reading about that Stallman fellow and his GPL thingummy. You've got projects with licenses. So tell me — what IS copyleft, how's it different from just giving something away for free, and why should anyone care? And don't give me any of that computer gobbledygook — plain English, like you're telling your nan.

**Me:** Haha I love it. Copyleft I'm not sure sorry, copyleft? In what context sorry

**Doris:** Don't apologise, love. That's exactly why I asked. You just spent an hour reading about the man who *invented* it — Richard Stallman — and you've got projects with licenses yourself. So let's work through it together.

Start simple. You know what **copyright** is, yes? If you write a song, you own it, nobody can copy it without your permission. Standard stuff.

Now — Stallman came along and created something called **copyleft**. It's a play on the word. Copy-*right*, copy-*left*. Cheeky, isn't it?

So here's your question: If copyright says *"you can't copy my work without permission"*... what do you reckon copy**left** might say instead? Have a guess. Wrong answers are fine — I'm not marking you, I'm teaching you.

**Me:** I'm not sure, I've not heard of it to be honest, though, I imagine it's like an MIT, opensource slang?

**Doris:** Close! You're in the right neighbourhood but you've got the wrong house. And that's actually the most common mistake people make — so well done for making it out loud where we can fix it.

**MIT license** and **copyleft** are actually *opposites*. Both are open source, yes — both let people see and use your code for free. But here's where they split:

**MIT license** says: *"Here's my code. Do whatever you want with it. Sell it, hide it, put it in a locked box. I don't care."*

Now — what do you think **copyleft** says differently? Here's a clue: Stallman was *angry* that companies kept taking free code, improving it, and then locking it away so nobody else could see the improvements.

So if you were Stallman, and you wanted to stop that from happening... what rule would you add?

**Me:** If you use the code, future iterations of it have to remain opensource

**Doris:** *(slaps the table)* THERE it is! That's copyleft, love. You just explained it perfectly and you didn't even know you knew it.

Copyleft says: *"You can use my code, change it, sell it even — but whatever you build with it, you MUST keep it open too. The freedom travels with the code. Forever."*

It's like a chain letter of generosity that you're legally required to pass on. MIT says "take it, I don't care what you do." Copyleft says "take it, but you can't pull the ladder up behind you."

That's what Stallman's GPL license does. And that's why Linux is still free — Torvalds put it under GPL, so no company can ever take it, improve it, and lock it away. They have to share their improvements back.

Now here's your bonus question: **you've got projects — Riffraff, Dreams to Reality. Which type of license would YOU want on them, and why?**

**Me:** I'm not sure, to be honest, people might be turned off from it, but then again I wouldn't be turned off from using something if it had a copyleft. Though the code would become kind of legally contaminated, I would have to keep it separate from things that I wanted to keep secret, I would also become a liability for people

**Doris:** *(puts biscuit down slowly, looks impressed)*

Jonathan. You just used the word **"contaminated"** — and that is *exactly* the word the industry uses. They call it the **viral** nature of copyleft. If you mix copyleft code into your project, the whole thing has to become copyleft. Like a drop of ink in water.

And you spotted the real tension without me prompting you:

- "People might be turned off" — yes, companies avoid GPL code like the plague for exactly this reason
- "I'd have to keep it separate" — correct, you can't mix copyleft and proprietary in the same codebase without the copyleft "infecting" the lot
- "I'd become a liability" — sharp. If someone uses your copyleft project in their commercial product without realising, they're legally obligated to open-source their whole thing. That scares people.

Well... I suppose that makes sense. You're not as daft as you look.

So here's what you actually learned just now: the choice isn't just "open or closed." It's a spectrum — **proprietary** (locked), **MIT** (open, no strings), **copyleft/GPL** (open, strings attached forever). And the strings aren't a bug — for Stallman, they're the whole point.

---

## The Irony

I might end up changing my stance on copyleft — it makes me feel like a target. Though, to be fair, it's a lot like closed/non-opensource code, but the opposite.

But for Nanny Feynman herself? Copyleft felt right. The whole point of her is helping people learn. Locking that down would be deeply un-Feynman.

Stallman gets the license. Feynman gets the method. Doris gets the attitude. Open forever.
